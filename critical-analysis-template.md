Here is a comprehensive list of topics and elements typically covered in a literary critical analysis, with special emphasis on how they are applied or adapted when the work belongs to **weird fiction** (Lovecraftian horror, cosmic horror, the New Weird, authors like Lovecraft, Ligotti, Caitlin R. Kiernan, China Miéville, Jeff VanderMeer, etc.).

### General Topics in Any Literary Critical Analysis
1. **Theme** – Central ideas, philosophical questions, messages  
2. **Narrative structure** – Plot, pacing, linearity vs. non-linearity, frame narratives  
3. **Point of view / Narrative voice** – First-person unreliability, third-person limited, epistolary forms  
4. **Characterisation** – Protagonist psychology, flat vs. round characters, archetypes  
5. **Setting** – Time, place, atmosphere, world-building  
6. **Symbolism and motif** – Recurring images, objects, colours, numbers  
7. **Tone and mood** – Emotional atmosphere (dread, awe, disgust, melancholy, etc.)  
8. **Style and language** – Diction, sentence structure, rhetorical devices, purple prose vs. minimalism  
9. **Genre conventions and subversion** – How the text adheres to or breaks genre rules  
10. **Intertextuality** – Allusions, references, pastiche, influences from other works  
11. **Historical / cultural context** – Author’s era, social anxieties reflected in the text  
12. **Authorial intent vs. reader response** – Death of the author, affective stylistics  
13. **Ideological critique** – Politics, race, gender, class, colonialism, environmentalism  
14. **Philosophical underpinnings** – Existentialism, nihilism, absurdism, phenomenology  

### Topics Especially Important or Distinctive in Weird Fiction Criticism
15. **Cosmic horror / the sublime** – Confrontation with the incomprehensible, Burkean/Kantian sublime, awe-terror  
16. **Cosmic insignificance (cosmicism)** – Humanity’s trivial place in the universe; annihilation of anthropocentrism  
17. **The unnameable / ineffability** – Limits of language, things that cannot be described or understood  
18. **Epistemological rupture** – Breakdown of rational knowledge, forbidden knowledge, madness as revelation  
19. **Ontological uncertainty** – Blurring of reality/fiction, dream/reality, human/non-human boundaries  
20. **Body horror and abjection** – Violation of the corporeal self, Kristevan abject, mutation, decay  
21. **Non-human agency and ecology** – Weird nature, hostile environments, post-human ecologies (New Weird especially)  
22. **Taboo knowledge and the occult** – Grimoires, cults, archaeology as dangerous uncovering  
23. **Temporal distortion** – Deep time, geological time, non-linear or cyclical time, entropy  
24. **Hybridity and the monstrous** – Monstrous hybrids, miscegenation fears (often problematic in older weird fiction), post-human bodies  
25. **Affective goals: dread, awe, disgust, fascination** – How the text produces specific weird affects rather than traditional horror scares  
26. **Unreliable narration and paranoia** – Narrators who descend into madness or whose sanity is questionable from the start  
27. **Materiality of the text** – Fungal books, infectious texts, texts that physically harm the reader (metafictional layer)  
28. **Postcolonial and racial anxieties** – Especially in Lovecraft: xenophobia, fear of the “other,” degenerate bloodlines; modern criticism often interrogates or subverts these  
29. **Weird materialism / object-oriented ontology** – Influence of speculative realism (Graham Harman, Eugene Thacker); things-that-should-not-be  
30. **The New Weird vs. Old Weird** – Political consciousness, urban settings, body mutation, anti-capitalist readings in Miéville, VanderMeer, etc.  
31. **Transgression and the taboo** – Violation of natural, social, sexual, and metaphysical laws  
32. **Atmosphere over plot** – Priority of mood, vibe, and “creep” rather than traditional narrative resolution  
33. **Anti-catharsis / refusal of closure** – Stories that end on unresolved dread or ontological destabilisation rather than reassurance  

These are the lenses most critics (academic or otherwise) will use when approaching a weird fiction text. A strong analysis usually combines several general literary tools with a handful of the weird-specific ones above, depending on whether the story leans more toward classic cosmic horror (Lovecraft, Blackwood), philosophical horror (Ligotti), or the politically charged New Weird (Miéville, VanderMeer).
