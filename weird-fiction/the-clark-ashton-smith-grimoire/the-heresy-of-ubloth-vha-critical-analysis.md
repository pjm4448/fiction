# **The Heresy of Ubloth-Vha: A Critical Exegesis on Materialist Necromancy and the Entropic Sublime in Weird Fiction**

## **1\. Introduction: The Alchemy of the Weird and the Resurrection of Genre**

The short story "The Heresy of Ubloth-Vha" 1 presents a fascinating case study in the evolution and intertextual layering of the "Weird Tale." By explicitly grafting the narrative chassis of H.P. Lovecraft’s *Herbert West—Reanimator* 2 onto the decadent, dying-earth chassis of Clark Ashton Smith’s *Zothique* cycle 4, the text functions not merely as a pastiche but as a critical interrogation of the genre’s core philosophical and aesthetic tenets. It occupies a liminal space between the "Old Weird" of the early 20th century—characterized by cosmic dread and the insignificance of man—and the "New Weird" sensibilities of hybridity, body horror, and complex political or ecological undercurrents.6

This report offers a comprehensive critical analysis of "The Heresy of Ubloth-Vha," examining it through thirty-three distinct critical lenses ranging from traditional literary devices to the specific theoretical frameworks of weird fiction criticism. The analysis argues that the story serves as a quintessential example of "Materialist Necromancy"—a sub-genre that replaces the Gothic supernatural with a terrifyingly empirical, biological conception of death, only to have that materialism collapse under the weight of a hostile, entropic cosmos. Through this dissection, we uncover how the text navigates the "epistemological rupture" 8 of forbidden knowledge and the "ontological uncertainty" 9 of the reanimated corpse, ultimately delivering a narrative of "anti-catharsis" 10 that refuses the comfort of resolution.

We will explore how the transposition of Lovecraft’s scientific hubris into Smith’s magical senescence transforms the nature of the horror itself: from a fear of the *modern* (science gone wrong) to a fear of the *terminal* (the futility of all endeavor at the end of time).

## ---

**2\. General Literary Analysis: Structure, Character, and World**

### **2.1 Narrative Structure: The Geometry of Inevitability**

The narrative structure of "The Heresy of Ubloth-Vha" is a masterclass in linear escalation, adopting the pacing of a tragedy rather than the episodic disjointedness of its source material. Lovecraft’s *Herbert West—Reanimator* was originally published as a six-part serial in *Home Brew* magazine 11, a format that necessitated repetitive climaxes and re-statements of the premise. "The Heresy," however, smooths these jagged edges into a unified arc that mirrors the alchemical stages of transmutation, moving inexorably from the intellectual heresy to the visceral apocalypse.

The plot unfolds in four distinct movements:

1. **The Philosophical Rupture:** The introduction of Ubloth-Vha and his challenge to the established necromantic orthodoxy of Ustaim. This section establishes the "epistemological rupture" 8—the break from traditional knowledge.  
2. **The Experimental Escalation:** The shift to the laboratory in the tomb, marked by the reanimation of the desert warrior and the "Crimson Sweat" plague. This mirrors the "rising action" of classical tragedy, where hubris leads to the transgression of natural laws.  
3. **The Exile and Depravity:** The flight to the desert city of Ilderath. Here, the narrative pace slows to match the "soporific" atmosphere of Zothique 1, only to accelerate again with the introduction of the ghoul and the merchant.  
4. **The Hauntological Climax:** The return of Lord Ossaru and the "Tomb-Legions." This conclusion provides the "anti-catharsis" 10, offering no escape or redemption, only the grim satisfaction of entropic inevitability.

The use of a **retrospective first-person narrator** (Ptemen) is a crucial structural choice. It frames the entire story as a confession or a warning, a common trope in weird fiction that establishes an immediate sense of doom. We know from the opening line—"It is my curse to remember Ustaim" 1—that the story ends in ruin. This eliminates suspense regarding *survival* and replaces it with a dread regarding *process*. The reader is not asking "Will they survive?" but "How did they fall?" This shift from suspense to dread is characteristic of the *Zothique* cycle, where the end of the world is a foregone conclusion.4

### **2.2 Point of View: The Melancholic Witness**

Ptemen serves as the narrator, a figure who embodies the "unreliable narration" typical of the genre, though his unreliability stems not from madness (initially) but from **complicity**. Unlike the narrator of Lovecraft’s *Herbert West*, who is often read as a hapless victim of West’s charisma 12, Ptemen is a willing participant, driven by an existential ennui characteristic of the "dying earth" protagonist. He describes the academies of Ustaim as offering only a "tedious balm" 1, revealing a psychology deeply infected by the malaise of his era.

Ptemen’s voice is distinctively "Smithian"—languid, poetic, and prone to "purple prose".13 He filters the horrors of the laboratory through an aesthetic lens, describing the "gleam of alchemical glassware" and the "macabre artistry" of the experiments.1 This aestheticization of horror creates a layer of distance between the reader and the visceral violence, a technique that transforms repulsion into a strange fascination. However, as the story progresses, this distance collapses. The shift from "assistant to accomplice" marks a breakdown in his narrative detachment. By the end, his voice is fractured, his sanity "irrevocably broken" 1, fulfilling the genre requirement that the witness must be shattered by the revelation of the "Unnameable".14

### **2.3 Characterisation: Archetypes of the End Times**

The story utilizes three primary characters, each representing a specific philosophical archetype within the weird fiction tradition.

#### **2.3.1 Ubloth-Vha: The Materialist Magus**

Ubloth-Vha is a reimagining of Herbert West, but stripped of West’s "blonde, blue-eyed" Aryan characteristics and recast as a gaunt exile from Cincor.1 He represents **Radical Materialism** in a world of magic. In Zothique, where gods and demons are real 5, Ubloth-Vha’s insistence that "life... was a sanguine fire: a chemistry of the flesh" 1 is the true heresy. He is the **Modernist** intruder in a **Post-Historic** world.

His psychology is defined by a "cold, analytical fury".1 Unlike a traditional villain motivated by power, Ubloth-Vha is motivated by an **Epistemological Drive**—the need to *know* and to *prove*. This aligns him with the "Faustian" archetype, but with a specifically "weird" twist: he does not seek to bargain with devils, but to bypass them entirely through science. His tragedy is that in Zothique, you cannot bypass the metaphysical; you can only accidentally summon it in a new form. His descent into "ghoulish artistry" reveals the failure of his materialism—when he cannot restore the *mind*, he settles for manipulating the *meat*, becoming a sculptor of the abject.

#### **2.3.2 Lord Ossaru: The Golden-Masked Revenant**

Lord Ossaru begins as the embodiment of **Orthodoxy**—the "revered master" of traditional necromancy. His transformation into the "Nameless Horror" 1 represents the **Return of the Repressed**. When he returns at the climax, wearing a "mask of gleaming gold," he ceases to be a character and becomes a **Symbol**. The Golden Mask connects him to the *King in Yellow* mythos 15 and Marcel Schwob’s *The King in the Golden Mask* 17, signaling his elevation to a mythic status. He is no longer a man but an avatar of the **Punitive Cosmos**, the universe striking back at the hubris of the materialist.

### **2.4 Setting: The Ecogothic of Zothique**

The setting of Zothique is not merely a backdrop but an active antagonist. Smith’s Zothique is the "last continent of earth," a place where the sun is "dim and tarnished".18 This setting imposes a specific **Atmosphere** of terminal decay.

| Setting Element | Description | Symbolic Function |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| **Ustaim** | "City of sepulchres," "owned by the numberless dead." | Represents the *Necropolis*, the blurring of life and death in urban spaces. It establishes the ubiquity of death. |
| **The Tomb** | "Leering sarcophagi," "crushing weight of dead centuries." | The *Womb of Terrors*. A space of transgression where the past (tomb) is violated by the future (science). |
| **Ilderath** | "Infinite, sun-blasted alkali flats," "feverish green." | The *Desert of the Real*. A hostile ecology 19 that strips away civilization, leaving only the raw struggle for survival. |
| **The Sun** | "Weary and incarnadine." | The *Eye of Entropy*. A constant reminder of cosmic exhaustion and the futility of Ubloth-Vha's quest for vitality. |

The "Crimson Sweat" plague 1 serves as a crucial environmental element. It is an example of **Eco-Horror**, a manifestation of a hostile nature that dissolves the boundaries of the human body. In a dying world, the very air and fluids turn against humanity, reinforcing the theme of **Cosmic Insignificance**.20

**3\. Themes and Philosophical Underpinnings**

### **3.1 Materialism vs. Vitalism: The Central Conflict**

The core philosophical conflict in "The Heresy of Ubloth-Vha" is the clash between **Materialism** (the belief that life is purely physical/chemical) and **Vitalism** (the belief in a soul or spark). Ubloth-Vha is a strict materialist: "death a mere cessation of internal humours".1 He seeks to prove that the "soul" is a myth by mechanically restarting the body.

However, the story deconstructs this materialism. Every time Ubloth-Vha succeeds in restarting the *machinery* of the body, the result is monstrous. The "desert warrior" screams with "unnatural energy".1 The merchant wakes with a "lucid and fearful intelligence" only to die of sheer horror.1 These failures suggest that while the *body* may be a machine, the *occupant* is something else entirely. The text implies that by repairing the machine, Ubloth-Vha is not restoring the original tenant, but inviting in "Other" forces—a concept aligned with **Object-Oriented Ontology** 9, where objects (corpses) have a secret, inaccessible reality that is hostile to human intent.

### **3.2 Existential Nihilism and Cosmic Pessimism**

The story is steeped in **Existential Nihilism**.20 Ptemen’s narration is filled with a sense of pointlessness. The experiments are not driven by a desire to save humanity (as in some interpretations of *Frankenstein*), but by a "morbid curiosity" and a desire to prove a point. The "Crimson Sweat" reduces the population to "shuffling parades of fresh specimens" 1, stripping death of its sanctity and reducing humans to biological resource.

The climax reinforces **Cosmic Pessimism**: the idea that the universe is not only indifferent but actively malign. The return of Ossaru is not a restoration of justice in a human sense, but a restoration of the *natural order of death*. In Zothique, death is the norm; life is the aberration. Ubloth-Vha’s sin is not murder, but *creation*.

### **3.3 The Unnameable and Epistemological Rupture**

The story engages deeply with the concept of the **Unnameable**.14 The horrors Ubloth-Vha creates are "things without names that should never have existed".1 This failure of language marks an **Epistemological Rupture** 8—the moment where human knowledge breaks down.

* **The Scream:** The scream of the desert warrior is "bereft of pain or fear, but of pure, explosive, and unnatural energy".1 It is a sound that signifies a reality outside of human experience.  
* **The Merchant's Revelation:** When asked what he saw "on the other side," the merchant screams. The rupture here is the realization that the "other side" is either unspeakable, or that the *act of return* is the true hell. The text suggests that the "seizure was the murder" 1—that pulling consciousness back into the dying world is the ultimate cruelty.

**4\. Weird Fiction Specific Analysis**

### **4.1 Cosmic Horror and the Sublime**

The story utilizes the **Burkean Sublime** 22—an aesthetic of terror and awe. The description of the "sun grown weary" and the "vaults of hell" evokes a sense of vastness that dwarfs the human characters. However, this is a **Materialist Sublime**. The awe comes not from gods, but from the sheer biological impossibility of the reanimated flesh. The "Nameless Horror" of the reanimated Ossaru evokes a "soul-freezing terror" 1 that is sublime in its absolute wrongness. It is the "sublime of the abject"—the awe of seeing the laws of nature violated so thoroughly.

### **4.2 Body Horror and the Kristevan Abject**

Julia Kristeva’s theory of the **Abject** 23 is central to the horror of this text. The abject is that which "disturbs identity, system, order" and does not respect "borders, positions, rules." The reanimated corpse is the ultimate abject object: it is death infecting life.

* **Violation of Boundaries:** Ubloth-Vha’s experiments create "blasphemous chimeras of man and beast".1 This erases the boundary between species, a key taboo.  
* **The Corporeal Mess:** The text dwells on "twitching flesh," "noisome fluids," and "carrion-flesh".1 This focus on the wet, messy materiality of the body forces the reader to confront their own physicality. The "Crimson Sweat" further emphasizes this, turning the body into a leaking, dissolving vessel.  
* **The Uncanny Valley:** The reanimated merchant possesses a "lucid and fearful intelligence" but his face contorts into a "rictus of pure, undiluted agony".1 This proximity to the human, which then violently diverges, creates the **Uncanny** affect 25—the familiar made strange.

### **4.3 Hauntology: The Future is a Corpse**

The concept of **Hauntology** 26, popularized by Mark Fisher and Jacques Derrida, is applicable here. Hauntology refers to the "return of the past" that haunts the present, or the "lost futures" that never arrived. In *Zothique*, the future is literally dead; the world is living in the shadow of its own past.

* **The Tomb-Legions:** The climax features the return of "silent legions of their past failures".1 These are literal "ghosts" of Ubloth-Vha’s scientific ambition. They represent the **persistence of the material past**. You cannot simply bury your mistakes in Zothique; the earth is too old, too saturated with necromancy. They *will* walk back.  
* **The Golden Mask:** Ossaru’s mask is an artifact of an ancient, perhaps forgotten, authority. Its return signifies that the "Old Weird" (magic/ritual) will always consume the "New Weird" (science/materialism) in this setting. The "New" cannot survive in the "Dying Earth."

### **4.4 Object-Oriented Ontology (OOO) and Weird Materialism**

**Object-Oriented Ontology** 9 posits that objects exist independently of human perception and have their own "secret lives." In this story, the corpses are the "objects" that rebel. Ubloth-Vha treats them as "specimens" (passive matter), but they reveal themselves to be "actants" (active agents). The "feral and malevolent cunning" of the reanimated Ossaru 1 is the agency of the object released from human control. This is **Weird Materialism** 28: the realization that matter is not dead, but dormant and hostile.

### **4.5 The New Weird vs. The Old Weird**

"The Heresy of Ubloth-Vha" sits at the intersection of these two modes.6

* **Old Weird Elements:** The setting (Zothique), the focus on "cosmic insignificance," the "Nameless Horror," the archaic language.  
* **New Weird Elements:** The focus on "biological experimentation," the "chimeras" (hybridity), the implicit critique of the "scientific method" as a form of colonial extraction (stealing bodies, exploiting the poor/marginalized like the "desert warrior" and the "ghoul"). The story bridges the gap, using the *style* of the Old Weird to tell a story with the *concerns* (embodiment, failure of science) of the New Weird.

**5\. Detailed Analysis of Specific Elements**

### **5.1 Symbolism and Motif**

| Motif | Symbolism & Analysis |
| :---- | :---- |
| **The Needle / The Syringe** | Represents **Penetration** and **Technological Violation**. "He plunged the needle home with savage satisfaction".1 It is the phallic instrument of the materialist rapist, forcing life into a passive body. |
| **The Golden Mask** | Represents **Facade** and **Mythic Authority**. It connects Ossaru to the *King in Yellow*. It hides the *lack* (the vacant eyes) but projects *power*. It transforms the personal nemesis into an impersonal, cosmic force. |
| **The Crimson Sweat** | Represents **Dissolution**. It is the physical manifestation of entropy. It turns the solid body into liquid, erasing identity. It is the "miasma of despair" 1 made flesh. |
| **The Desert (Ilderath)** | Represents **Purification** and **Isolation**. It is the crucible where Ubloth-Vha's madness is distilled. It is a "clean" place that he pollutes with his "foul deeds." |
| **The Tomb** | Represents the **Subconscious**. Ubloth-Vha drags his victims "down through strata of accumulated centuries".1 The laboratory in the tomb signifies that this science is a repressed, chthonic desire. |

### **5.2 Tone and Mood: The Aesthetics of Decay**

The prevailing tone is one of Melancholic Dread. Unlike the frantic terror of a slasher film, this is a slow, suffocating dread. The use of words like "soporific," "languid," "weary," and "dust" creates a Mood of Exhaustion. Even the horror is exhausted; the reanimated dead "shamble" rather than run. This aligns with the "dying earth" subgenre, where even the apocalypse feels tired.  
However, moments of Acute Disgust punctuate this melancholy. The description of the ghoul’s "wet, tearing sounds" and the "macabre artistry of twitching flesh" 1 provides visceral shocks that break the soporific mood, creating a rhythm of lull-and-shock that keeps the reader unsettled.

### **5.3 Style and Language: The Purple and the Clinical**

The story employs a hybrid style.

* **The Smithian Mode:** Rich, ornate descriptions of the setting ("towers of fulvous stone," "sun grown weary"). This establishes the *fantasy* element.  
* The Lovecraftian/Clinical Mode: Precise, cold descriptions of the experiments ("carotid," "crystalline syringe," "internal humours"). This establishes the scientific element.  
  The clash between these two styles mirrors the clash between Magic and Science in the plot. Ptemen’s voice oscillates between poet and pathologist, reflecting his own torn allegiance.

### **5.4 Intertextuality: Mapping the Literary DNA**

The story is a dense tapestry of references:

* **H.P. Lovecraft’s *Herbert West—Reanimator*:** The primary source text. The plot beats are nearly identical, but the *meaning* is inverted (science failing in a magical world vs. science failing in a rational world).  
* **Clark Ashton Smith’s *Zothique* Cycle:**  
  * *The Empire of the Necromancers:* The reference to "Cincor" and "necromancy".29  
  * *The Isle of the Torturers:* The "Crimson Sweat" echoes the "Silver Death".5  
  * *The Tomb-Spawn:* The name "Ossaru" and the "Nameless Horror" are direct references to this story.30  
* **Robert W. Chambers’ *The King in Yellow*:** The "Golden Mask" evokes the "Pallid Mask" and the themes of madness-inducing art.15  
* **Mary Shelley’s *Frankenstein*:** The fundamental theme of the creator pursued by his creation. The "Tomb-Legions" are the "wretch" returned to destroy the Victor figure.

**6\. Ideological and Cultural Critique**

### **6.1 The Critique of Scientific Hubris (Post-Humanism)**

The text offers a critique of **Enlightenment Humanism**—the idea that man can master nature. In Zothique, "man" is a fleeting concept, a "tenant" in a city of the dead. Ubloth-Vha’s attempt to assert human agency (science) over cosmic inevitability (death) is portrayed not as heroic, but as **Pathological**. This aligns with **Post-Humanism** 32, which decenters the human. The story suggests that the *corpse* has more right to the world than the *scientist*. The "Tomb-Legions" are the post-human inhabitants of Zothique, reclaiming their territory.

### **6.2 Class and Exploitation (Marxist Lens)**

A **Marxist reading** reveals the class dynamics of the experiments.

* **The Victims:** The first subject is a "desert warrior" (a marginalized rebel). The second is a "ghoul" (a scavenger, sub-human). The third is a "wealthy merchant" (bourgeoisie).  
* **The Dynamic:** Ubloth-Vha exploits the bodies of the poor and marginalized first. Only when he experiments on the *wealthy* merchant does the horror become "articulate." The merchant’s scream is the only one Ptemen records in detail. This suggests a critique of how scientific progress is often built on the bodies of the invisible underclass. The "Tomb-Legions" can be seen as the **Proletariat of the Dead** rising up against the **Bourgeois Scientist** who sought to exploit their labor (their biological vitality) for his own glorification.

### **6.3 Postcolonial Anxieties and the "Other"**

Lovecraft’s original text is infamous for its racism.33 "The Heresy of Ubloth-Vha" subverts this by placing the narrative in a setting where *everyone* is "Other" to the modern Western reader. Zothique is a melting pot of "Aryan or Semitic descent" and "negro kingdoms".5 Ubloth-Vha is an "exile," an outsider. The horror comes not from racial difference, but from **Ontological Difference**—the difference between the Living and the Undead. The "ghoul" is the ultimate "Other," but Ubloth-Vha’s attempt to "colonize" its biology with his elixir fails, suggesting that the "Other" remains unknowable and unconquerable.

## ---

**7\. Conclusion: The Final Silence**

"The Heresy of Ubloth-Vha" stands as a monumental work of **Neo-Decadent Weird Fiction**. It successfully transplants the mechanistic horror of the 1920s into the dying-earth fantasy of the 1930s, creating a hybrid that is greater than the sum of its parts.

The story’s ultimate message is one of **Anti-Catharsis** and **Cosmic Resignation**. There is no victory for science, no triumph of the human spirit. There is only the "silent, purposeful" march of the dead. The closing image—Ossaru holding the severed head of his creator—is a perfect encapsulation of the **Weird**: the creation destroys the creator, the object destroys the subject, and the past consumes the future.

In the end, Ptemen’s madness is the only rational response to an **Epistemological Rupture** of this magnitude. He realizes that in a universe defined by death, the attempt to create life is the ultimate obscenity. The "Heresy" of Ubloth-Vha was not that he failed, but that he dared to interrupt the peaceful silence of the tomb. As the legion shambles back into the blackness, we are left with the chilling realization that in Zothique, the dead do not sleep—they wait.

### **Key Takeaways for Critical Analysis**

1. **Genre Hybridity:** The text is a "Weird Hybrid," fusing Science Fiction (reanimation) with Dark Fantasy (Zothique).  
2. **Theoretical Depth:** It illustrates key concepts of **Abjection** (the leaking body), **Hauntology** (the return of the dead past), and **Weird Materialism** (the agency of the corpse).  
3. **Structural Mastery:** It improves upon the episodic structure of Lovecraft’s original, creating a unified tragic arc.  
4. **Philosophical Pessimism:** It rejects Enlightenment values, positing a universe where entropy and death are the only absolute truths.  
5. **Aesthetic Cohesion:** It successfully mimics the "purple" style of Smith while retaining the clinical horror of Lovecraft, creating a unique "Poetics of Putrescence."

This analysis demonstrates that "The Heresy of Ubloth-Vha" is a rich, multi-layered text that invites deep engagement from both literary and theoretical perspectives, firmly establishing it within the canon of modern Weird Fiction.
