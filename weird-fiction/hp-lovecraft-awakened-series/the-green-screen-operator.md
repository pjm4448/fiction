# The Green Screen Operator

**Summary**

In The Green Screen Operator, a chilling blend of historical intrigue and cosmic horror, CIA analyst Michael Davies uncovers unsettling anomalies in 1985’s covert operations. Tasked with tracking Contra funding, he stumbles upon financial trails tied to obscure cults and celestial alignments. As Oliver North orchestrates secret arms deals with Iran, Davies’s data reveals a deeper, sinister force manipulating events. Glitches, whispers, and nightmares haunt his investigation, hinting at an otherworldly influence guiding the Iran-Contra affair. Will Davies expose the truth, or be silenced by a reality too terrifying to comprehend?

## Part 1: The Gathering Static

### Chapter 1: Flores and Filters

(Spring 1985\)

The hum was the first thing you noticed, or rather, the first thing you learned to stop noticing. It wasn't just one hum, but a layered symphony of low-frequency sound that permeated the seventh floor of the CIA's Original Headquarters Building at Langley. The ballast in the endless banks of fluorescent lights buzzed with a sixty-cycle thrum, punctuated by the whirring fans of countless computer terminals and the distant, rhythmic clatter of dot-matrix printers. Add the perpetual white noise pumped through the ventilation – designed, Michael Davies assumed, to mask conversations as much as circulate air – and you had the ambient soundtrack to American intelligence gathering, circa 1985\.

Davies leaned back in his government-issue chair, the faux-leather cracking. He ran a hand through his already untidy brown hair and squinted at the IBM 3278 terminal, its screen glowing with sharp, impossibly green characters against an absolute black. His current screen was a dense wall of numbers and Cyrillic acronyms – Soviet state enterprise budgets. His job: follow the money, find the tells in the vast flow of Rubles and resources. It was meticulous, often tedious work.

He sipped lukewarm coffee. Outside his cramped cubicle, the section felt subdued. Just the hum, the click of keyboards, and the rustle of paper. Davies had been with the Agency for five years, recruited out of Georgetown. He’d chosen Soviet Finance because it felt like the front line of the Cold War. He liked the precision, the logic puzzle.

He toggled a function key. Time to run the latest cross-correlation matrix. EXEC SOVFIN\_CORRELATE\_ENERTRANS\_Q1\_85. Enter. JOB SUBMITTED. QUEUE POSITION: 14\. ESTIMATED WAIT TIME: 45 MINUTES. Davies sighed. Mainframe traffic was heavy.

As he stood, his section chief, Bill Thompson, appeared. Thompson was a career man, mid-fifties, grey suit, grey tie, grey complexion. "Davies," Thompson said, his voice low. "Got a minute?"

"Sure, Bill. Just waiting on the mainframe."

Thompson nodded, holding a thin manila folder. "Something new. Sideways from my usual taskings, but Casey’s putting pressure on everything related to Central America. Needs eyes on some... non-traditional funding streams."

Davies raised an eyebrow. Central America wasn't his usual playground. The Contra funding debate was constantly in the news, Congress tying itself in knots with the Boland Amendment. "Non-traditional?"

"Private donations, third-country pass-throughs, maybe some creative accounting," Thompson said vaguely. "Directorate of Operations is handling HUMINT, S\&T is looking at signals, but someone upstairs wants Finance to take a pass. Low priority. Fit it in. Report anything anomalous directly to me, off the regular system. Understand?" Thompson’s eyes held a flicker of something Davies couldn’t quite place – weariness, or perhaps a deeper, unstated concern.

"Understood, Bill," Davies said, taking the folder. "Keep it compartmentalized."

"Casey's obsessed," Thompson muttered, rubbing his temples. "Thinks Nicaragua is the key. Pushing hard." He gave Davies a curt nod. "Just... keep your head down and run the numbers."

Davies looked at the folder. Three pages. The first listed half a dozen non-profit organizations sympathetic to the Contra cause. The second listed account structures – shell corporations, layered ownerships, banks in Panama, the Caymans. Standard.

The third page was odder. Several seemingly unrelated entities: a historical preservation society in New Orleans, an import/export company dealing in antique textiles, a small religious charity. Below each, complex strings of numbers – possibly account identifiers, but in unfamiliar formats. No explanations.

He powered up the Compaq Portable beside his main terminal. Popping in a floppy disk labelled "FINTOOLS," he booted into MS-DOS. He launched Lotus 1-2-3 and started inputting the names and account details, linking into the Agency's financial tracking database via a secure modem – a slow, screeching connection.

For the next hour, Davies worked, cross-referencing the known groups. Much was legitimate. Nothing screamed illegal Contra funding, though the potential was there.

Then he started on the strange list. The New Orleans historical society: minimal activity. The textile importer: more traffic, wires from South America and Europe, amounts consistent with legitimate trade. The Caribbean charity: barely active.

He tried inputting the long numeric strings. Most returned errors: INVALID FORMAT or NO MATCH FOUND. Except one, under the New Orleans historical society. After a long pause, the modem spitting static, the database returned a hit. Not an account record, but a cross-reference flag to a decades-old, archived counter-intelligence file. The designation was cryptic, heavily redacted. Unredacted keywords: CULT ACTIVITY \- LOUISIANA, MARITIME INCIDENT \- PACIFIC, UNSUBSTANTIATED.

Davies stared. Cult activity? What did a New Orleans historical society have to do with an old CI file mentioning cults? Must be data corruption. He made a note – NOLA HPS \- NUMSTR\#4 \- ANOMALOUS DB LINK \- CHECK ARCHIVE REQ? – then decided against it. Chasing phantoms was a waste of time.

He saved the spreadsheet to a floppy disk: PROJECT NIGHTJAR \- PRELIM. His mainframe queue position was finally 1\. The screen flickered, then filled with Soviet correlation results. Davies pushed the unsettling database glitch from his mind and leaned forward, ready to dive back into the cold, rational calculus of the Cold War. But somewhere beneath the hum, a different kind of static seemed to linger, faint and almost entirely unnoticed.

### Chapter 2: Whispers from Tehran

(Summer 1985\)

The Situation Room felt like a pressure cooker. Fluorescent lights beat down on the polished mahogany table, reflecting strained faces. Robert McFarlane, National Security Advisor, felt the weight of eight American hostages in Lebanon.

Across the table, Vice President Bush listened intently. CIA Director Casey, his face a craggy landscape, leaned back, an observer. "The latest from our intermediaries," McFarlane said, "is... inconclusive. Hezbollah demands the release of their seventeen compatriots in Israeli custody. The Israelis are adamant: no negotiations."

"A policy getting our people killed," Oliver North interjected, impatient. The Marine Lieutenant Colonel, from the NSC staff, radiated restless energy.

"We understand your frustration, Colonel," McFarlane said, "but we must proceed with caution."

"With all due respect," North said, "we've been cautious for months. We're getting nowhere. These people understand only strength."

"And how do you propose we do that, Colonel?" Bush asked, his voice smooth but steely.

North spread his papers. "Intelligence indicates... moderate elements within Iran may be willing to exert influence on Hezbollah. They might be open to a quid pro quo."

"The Iranians?" Bush raised an eyebrow. "Negotiate with Tehran?"

"Not directly, sir," North clarified. "A back channel. An exchange of... incentives. We provide something they need, they use their influence."

"Incentives," McFarlane repeated, tasting bitterness. Weapons. It violated every principle.

"It's a long shot," North conceded, "but the only one we haven't taken."

McFarlane looked at Casey. The CIA Director's expression was unreadable. He seemed to study North with detached interest, his eyes holding a depth that suggested knowledge of far older, more convoluted games. "The President is... intrigued," Casey said finally, his voice a low rumble. "He believes we should explore all avenues." Casey’s gaze flickered towards the President, a subtle, almost imperceptible acknowledgment, as if he were merely confirming a pre-ordained script.

McFarlane felt a cold dread. Intrigued was an understatement. The President's recent impatience, his strange certainty about this high-risk plan, wasn't usual Reagan optimism. It was something harder, more driven.

"Mr. President," McFarlane began, "I have grave reservations. We'd be violating our own principles."

Ronald Reagan sat at the head of the table, his gaze distant, as if listening to a conversation only he could hear. When he spoke, his voice was calm, almost serene, but with an underlying intensity. "Bob," Reagan said, "I understand your concerns. But we cannot stand idly by. Sometimes, we have to make difficult choices. Be... pragmatic." He paused, a slight smile that didn't reach his eyes. "I have a feeling about this, Bob. A strong feeling. This... opportunity... it's a sign. We need to pursue it."

McFarlane felt a chill. It wasn't the words, but the almost hypnotic certainty. As if the President were following a different logic, guided by a different force.

"But Mr. President," McFarlane pressed, "the risks..."

Reagan waved a hand dismissively. "We can manage the risks, Bob. We have capable people. People like Colonel North." He glanced at North.

North straightened. "I can make this work, sir. I have contacts, resources."

McFarlane saw the same unnerving certainty on both faces. He felt a wave of nausea. The hum of the lights seemed to intensify. He was losing. "Very well, Mr. President," McFarlane said, defeated. "But I want it on record: I have serious reservations."

Reagan smiled that unsettling smile. "Don't worry, Bob. Everything will be fine. We just need to... have a little faith."

As McFarlane left, the hum seemed to follow him. He felt they weren't just making a political gamble. They were opening a door to something far more sinister.

### Chapter 3: The Phoenix Program

(August \- September 1985\)

Oliver North lived on coffee, adrenaline, and the conviction he was doing God’s work. Or the President’s. His OEOB office was a controlled explosion of paper. The air hummed with his IBM PC/XT and the incessant STU-II telephone.

Since the President’s green light, North had thrown himself into Operation Recovery – arms for hostages. The first phase: Israel as intermediary to ship HAWK missiles to Iran. Officially, an arms embargo. Unofficially, North orchestrated a shell game.

He hunched over his keyboard, tapping a coded message. Funds from an Iranian contact to Adnan Khashoggi’s Zurich account, then to Israel for the HAWKs. Convoluted, risky, probably illegal, but North felt a fierce certainty.

The STU-II warbled. "North."

"Ollie, it's Ami," said Amiram Nir, Israeli counter-terrorism advisor. "Initial deposit confirmed. Less than anticipated but confirmed."

"Less? How much less?" North scribbled furiously.

"Enough to proceed. The birds will fly. But tell your contacts their accounting needs work."

"I'll handle it," North said. "Ensure those HAWKs arrive precisely."

"Of course. Though the flight path requested by your... friends... is unusual. Over some rather empty desert. Certain coordinates seem almost… geometrically significant."

North frowned. He hadn't specified a flight path. Ghorbanifar, the intermediary? "Just stick to the plan, Ami."

"As you wish." Click.

North hung up. Query Ghorbanifar re: FLIGHT PATH? Probably nothing. He turned back to his computer, initiating another transfer – operational cash for the Contras, from an account linked to a veterans' support group, one listed in Thompson's "Project Nightjar" parameters. Routing codes from Casey’s office – long, complex strings. As he hit Enter, the screen flickered, a flash of interwoven geometric lines, sharp and unsettling, before resolving to TRANSACTION COMPLETE. North blinked. Too much coffee. Screen glare. Headaches lately. Stress.

Miles away, Michael Davies was running checks on the Panamanian corporation that received North's transfer. Flagged under "Project Nightjar." A sudden influx from an account linked to the New Orleans historical society and the cult file. The amount wasn't huge, but the timing and source were odd. He traced it to a numbered Swiss account. But the originating flag… it felt wrong. Static on a clean signal.

He typed an encrypted note: PROJECT NIGHTJAR \- PANAMA CORP \#P-1138 \- INFLUX FROM ACCT LINKED NOLA HPS FLAG (CF. CI FILE REFS CULT/MARITIME INCIDENT). POSSIBLE CO-MINGLING? He paused. Thompson said low priority. Mentioning the cult file would sound cranky. He closed the log. Likely nothing.

But as North worked late, he couldn't shake the feeling he was handling something volatile, leaving a strange residue. The headaches, flickering screens, the President's unnerving certainty – just noise. Static. Weren't they?

### Chapter 4: Green Screens and Ghost Money

(October 1985\)

Autumn settled over Langley, but inside Michael Davies’s world, it was perpetually springtime for the phosphor glow. The “Project Nightjar” tasking had become an insistent itch. The anomaly – funds flowing into Panamanian shell \#P-1138 from an account linked to the New Orleans Historical Society and the "Cult Activity" file – had burrowed under his skin.

He dedicated downtime, and his own time, to the Compaq Portable. Spreadsheets cross-referencing Thompson's list against wider databases. Patterns emerged, but not expected ones. Money flowed to unexpected places – a Miami textile importer (on Thompson’s third page) received large payments for "antique Andean textiles" valued far above market price. The money then went to accounts for "cultural exchange programs" or "private security consultants."

He mapped the flow geographically. Instead of a spiderweb on Central America, the map looked… wrong. Certain cities – Lima, New Orleans, even Reykjavik – were disproportionate nodes. Chronologically connected dots sometimes formed sharp, almost crystalline geometric shapes, angles too precise for laundered money. He shook his head. Pareidolia. Yet, the regularity of some patterns, specific obscure routing codes, nagged him.

Technology wasn't helping. Floppy disks failed: SECTOR NOT FOUND. Once, saving an analysis, the Compaq froze. Rebooting, the file wasn't just corrupted; parts contained Wingdings-like symbols mixed with fractured Cyrillic – meaningless junk, but unsettling.

Secure phone lines weren’t immune. On a STU-II call, a burst of high-frequency static. Twice, within it, he thought he heard something else – faint, rhythmic clicking or popping, almost primitive drumming or distorted speech, before the line cleared. Stress, he told himself. Auditory hallucination.

He started keeping his blinds slightly more closed. Listened to The Cure or New Order on his Walkman to drown out the office hum and, he hoped, the phantom static.

One rainy Tuesday, he tried accessing the original archived counter-intelligence file again – "Cult Activity \- Louisiana." Two days later, a terse, encrypted message from Records Management: REQUEST DENIED. FILE CLASSIFIED LEVEL DELTA. REQUIRES DCIA AUTHORIZATION. FURTHER INQUIRIES DISCOURAGED. Level Delta? Casey himself would sign off. Discouraged? A warning.

Davies felt fear and frustration. Why was a file linked to a sleepy historical society locked down so tight? It made the financial connections feel less random, more deliberately obscured. He was just a numbers guy. But Project Nightjar facts refused any rational intelligence model. Like trying to map a coastline that kept changing shape. He saved his work. For today, done chasing ghost money. He needed reality. But the phantom patterns, glitches, whispers in the static – they lingered.

### Chapter 5: The Admiral's Watch

(December 1985\)

When Robert McFarlane resigned as National Security Advisor in early December, it sent a jolt through the West Wing. Publicly, a voluntary departure. Privately, everyone knew Bud was burned out, exhausted by internal battles, the hostage crisis, and risky covert operations.

On his last day, McFarlane paused by Oliver North’s cluttered office. "Keep your head down, Ollie," he said, voice low. "Be careful who you trust."

North, looking up from telexes, offered a brisk smile. "Don't worry, sir. We're making progress."

McFarlane nodded slowly, his eyes lingering on North's fervent gaze. He felt an urge to warn North about the strange pressure, the dark currents, but words wouldn't form.

His replacement: Vice Admiral John Poindexter, McFarlane’s former deputy. Calm, naval discipline. Quiet, meticulous, eyes obscured behind thick glasses. Spoke in precise, clipped tones. The atmosphere shifted. Freewheeling spirit replaced by rigid, hierarchical structure.

North adapted quickly, energized by Poindexter’s demand for process. Shortly after Poindexter took the helm, North presented an idea: "Admiral, funds from the... ah... Iranian outreach is insufficient for robust support of Nicaraguan resistance. We could significantly enhance support by diverting residual profits from future arms sales directly to the democratic resistance."

Poindexter listened impassively. "The President is committed to supporting the Nicaraguan freedom fighters," he stated finally. "Ensure absolute discretion. No U.S. Government fingerprints."

"Yes, sir," North said, triumphant. "I've identified secure financial channels." He thought of the Panamanian corporations, Swiss accounts – channels Davies unknowingly monitored.

"Good. Now, operational security." Poindexter slid a folder across. "New protocols. Specific encryption keys, designated secure comms windows, revised code word usage. Sensitive hardcopy in burn bags."

North took the folder. Complex procedures, far more stringent. "Understood, Admiral."

"Indeed, Colonel." A faint, almost imperceptible smile. "We must ensure the signal remains clear, free from external noise."

As North left, he felt renewed purpose. Poindexter's cool efficiency felt right. He didn't know the new encryption keys resonated with non-Euclidean geometries, or that secure comms windows coincided with times conducive to psychic influence. He didn't know the Admiral’s secrecy served not only earthly discovery, but also to insulate and amplify subtle, guiding signals from the true operator, ensuring the Symphony of Madness proceeded, shielded by bureaucracy and the Admiral’s chilling calm.

## Part 2: The Signal Amplified

### Chapter 6: Presidential Finding

(January 17, 1986\)

The Resolute Desk felt solid beneath Ronald Reagan’s hands. Sunlight streamed into the Oval Office. Across stood Secretary of State George Shultz and Secretary of Defence Caspar Weinberger, faces etched with concern. Behind them, Admiral John Poindexter, impassive. Chief of Staff Donald Regan hovered.

The document: an Intelligence Finding, authorizing direct U.S. arms sales to Iran. It invoked national security, engaging Iranian moderates, freeing hostages.

"Mr. President," Shultz began, "I reiterate profound objections. Selling arms to Khomeini's Iran undermines everything."

Weinberger agreed. "Strategic risks far outweigh potential gains."

Reagan listened patiently, his warm smile firm. He respected both men. But they couldn't feel what he felt. A profound, unshakeable certainty. Guidance, a clear signal. This is the way. Open the door. The opportunity is fleeting. He trusted that feeling more than any advisor.

"George, Cap," Reagan said, "I value your counsel. But American lives hang in the balance. Sometimes, you take a calculated risk." He looked at the Finding. Poindexter had drafted it masterfully. Language precise, legally sound, yet phrases resonated with Reagan’s inner certainty: "contingency," "covert methodologies," "facilitate unforeseen opportunities," "strategic alignment." They felt… charged. Poindexter had an uncanny knack.

"Admiral Poindexter assures me," Reagan continued, "operational security will be absolute. A temporary measure, to build trust."

Shultz and Weinberger exchanged weary looks. Once the President had that serene conviction, argument was futile.

"We have registered our objections, Mr. President," Shultz said.

"Duly noted, George."

He picked up the pen. The act felt ceremonial. As he lowered it, that inner certainty surged. Arguments seemed distant. This action felt necessary, a key turning in a lock only he could see. He was enacting part of a larger plan, guided by an intuition truer than any intelligence briefing. His signature flowed: Ronald Reagan .

He capped the pen, meeting Poindexter's gaze. The Admiral gave a slight, almost imperceptible nod. "Thank you, Mr. President," Poindexter said, retrieving the Finding. "Necessary actions will proceed."

Reagan leaned back, feeling profound satisfaction. The path forward was clear.

### Chapter 7: TOWs and Troubles

(February \- May 1986\)

With the Presidential Finding signed, Operation Recovery shifted into high gear. Objective: move TOW anti-tank missiles to Iran for hostages. Oliver North remained the architect, conductor, and frayed nerve centre.

His office became a 24-hour command post. Sleep was short bursts on a battered couch. Diet: lukewarm coffee, stale doughnuts, Marlboros. The STU-II phone seemed permanent. Logistics were staggering: secure flights, discreet missile pallets, coordinating payments through Lake Resources Inc. and other fronts by Richard Secord and Albert Hakim. North managed the diversion, siphoning profits to the Contras. Spinning plates on poles during an earthquake, but North thrived on it.

Pressure left cracks. Manic intensity mixed with sharp exhaustion. Tempers flared. Headaches were constant. Nights offered no escape. Vivid nightmares: vast, impossible cities under sickly green skies, geometric angles that hurt, buildings twisting like liquid stone. Frantic, rhythmic drumming. Unseen, colossal shapes. Stress, he told himself. Push through. He tried to rationalize the images, but a chilling familiarity to them, as if they were memories from a life not his own, or whispers from a source he dared not name, pricked at the edges of his consciousness.

First TOW shipment: February. Another: May. Each brought adrenaline, momentary vindication. But hostage releases were slow, piecemeal. Reverend Jenco was released in July, but Frank Reed was taken captive. Ghorbanifar offered excuses, demanded more. "Moderate elements" seemed powerless or duplicitous. North pushed harder, convinced the next deal would be the breakthrough.

At Langley, Michael Davies watched financial flows accelerate. Since early in the year, Project Nightjar accounts, like Lake Resources, became hyperactive. Millions from "Iranian payments" flowed out, split between European arms brokers and the Contra support network. The diversion was there in transaction logs, hidden by corporate camouflage.

Volume was staggering. Davies documented it. He noticed a change in accounts for Contra funding. New entities: "Udall Research Corp," "Stanford Technology Trading Group International." More disturbingly, some transactions routed through accounts whose signatory names, cross-referenced against deep historical or linguistic databases, pinged against obscure mythologies or pre-Akkadian dialects. Names deliberately archaic, resonant with the "Cult Activity" file.

One shell corp, "Korel Limited," channeling funds to a Central American air charter, linked to folklore of subterranean beings, madness, decay. Coincidence? But the pattern of strangeness chipped at Davies’s scepticism.

He left his Walkman off, straining for phantom static, half-convinced he could decipher rhythmic clicks. He’d catch himself tracing strange geometric transaction maps, feeling vertigo. He was seeing pieces of something vast, deeply wrong. The TOWs flew, money moved, the President committed. But Davies felt the real cargo wasn't just weapons or cash. Something intangible, corrosive, spreading through wires and encrypted signals like a contagion, leaving static, nightmares, and the cold glow of the green screen.

### Chapter 8: The Tehran Cake

(May 25-28, 1986\)

The unmarked Boeing 707 hurtled towards Tehran. Oliver North stared out at the dark landscape. Beside him, Robert McFarlane, back as a consultant, fidgeted nervously. McFarlane looked terrible: pale, drawn, eyes darting. North attributed it to pressure, risks.

Delegation: George Cave (ex-CIA, Farsi fluent), Howard Teicher (NSC), Amiram Nir (Israeli, forged Irish passport). Cargo: HAWK missile spare parts, and a large chocolate cake from Tel Aviv, shaped like a key – a symbolic offering. Absurdly theatrical.

Landing at Mehrabad Airport was tense. Met by second-tier functionaries. Air felt heavy, ancient, stagnant. Revolutionary Guards watched with suspicion. Driven in curtained cars to the Esteghlal (former Hilton) Hotel. Passports confiscated, movements restricted. Oppressive atmosphere. Khomeini portraits stared ubiquitously. North felt a constant hum of tension, watched by more than human eyes.

Promised meetings with high-level "moderates" like Speaker Rafsanjani failed. Days of frustrating talks with mid-level functionaries. Impossible demands: Dawa prisoners released, US support for Iraq withdrawn, vast weaponry. Uninterested in missile parts, dismissive of the cake, indifferent to hostages.

McFarlane grew agitated, pacing, arguments sharp, desperate. "They lied, Ollie," he muttered. "Ghorbanifar lied. No moderates. Just... this." He gestured at the dark, alien city.

North, frustrated, tried to remain focused. He noticed small details. An official absently traced a complex, star-like pattern on the table. Another wore a ring with an unusually carved stone, its facets catching light, making North momentarily dizzy. In one meeting room, an old Persian tapestry: swirling, chaotic battle between monstrous, tentacled creatures and figures in ancient armour – disturbing, out of place. Cultural oddities, he dismissed. But they added to the unease.

Third day, McFarlane broke. "I... I need some air," he stammered, stumbling out, supported by Cave.

Left with Teicher and impassive Iranians, North felt defiant. "We came in good faith. If you are serious, now is the time. We will not be played for fools."

One Iranian official, with the strange ring, smiled faintly, cold, humourless. "Faith, Colonel, is complex. Powers far older than your nation, or ours. Arrangements made. Payments rendered. Necessary... adjustments... proceed. The wheel turns. What is desired will be obtained. Your presence is merely... commentary." His finger tapped the star-pattern.

Words made no literal sense, yet sent a chill. A glimpse behind a curtain, a different negotiation on a different level. He pushed it away – cryptic Iranian tactics.

Mission collapsed. Leaving Tehran felt like surfacing from underwater. McFarlane was quiet, staring blankly. North felt failure, frustration, lingering unease. He'd seen... something... in Tehran. In art, gestures, cryptic words. Filed under "cultural differences." No room for phantom fears.

### Chapter 9: Static on the Line

(Summer 1986\)

Washington D.C. summer: damp, sweltering. For Michael Davies, the oppressive atmosphere wasn't just meteorological. Project Nightjar became an obsession. Failed Tehran mission seemed to intensify strange financial flows. More money, faster, through more convoluted routes.

He correlated transaction dates temporally, cross-referencing against astronomical data. Disturbing frequency: significant fund movements, especially involving New Orleans Historical Society flag or Miami textile importer, coincided with minor lunar phases, specific planetary conjunctions, obscure meteor showers. Statistically anomalous. Why would covert transactions align with celestial events? Control tests on unrelated datasets showed no correlation. Only Nightjar danced to the rhythm of the spheres.

He took findings (statistical anomalies only) to Bill Thompson. Thompson listened patiently. “Interesting correlations, Davies. But… correlations aren’t causation. Coincidence. Artifact of reporting schedules.” He handed printouts back. “This Nightjar thing is sensitive. Casey’s office is… protective. My advice? Stick to primary tasking. Soviets. Don’t chase patterns. Not helpful for your career.” Pause. “Understood?” Clear warning: Drop it . Davies nodded. “Understood, Bill.”

He couldn’t. Patterns too strange, glitches too persistent. July afternoon, deep search query, screen blanked. Not usual error, pure black. Then, new text scrolled:

SYMBOLICS 3600 LISP MACHINE ENVIRONMENT (SERIAL \#00-4B7)

INITIALIZING ZETALISP KERNEL...

CHECKING FRANZ LISP COMPATIBILITY...

LOADING FLAVORS SYSTEM...

ERROR: NON-EUCLIDEAN STACK OVERFLOW. ATTEMPTING RECURSIVE HEALING.

Davies’s blood ran cold. Lisp Machine? Symbolics? High-end AI hardware, not for CIA mainframe terminals. Non-Euclidean Stack Overflow ? Computer science jargon filtered through a nightmare. Screen blanked, returned to login. No error logs.

Dizzy, breathless. Had he imagined it? He started documenting everything in a spiral notebook: glitch dates/times, Lisp message text, celestial correlations, odd account names ("Korel Limited"), cult file reference. Felt like Professor Angell from that obscure paper he’d found.

Finding that paper: another strange event. Searching pre-Akkadian dialects (obsessive curiosity), used an early inter-university network. Deep in Miskatonic University’s dusty academic archive server (real place, esoteric library), a scanned monograph: "Predatory Eschatological Cults Among Præ-Semitic Peoples" by Prof. Emeritus William Channing Angell, Brown University, 1926\. Dry linguistics, but sections discussed recurring symbols (star-patterns like the Iranian official traced), cosmic entities from "beyond the stars," and crucially, correlations between ritual activities and specific, cyclical celestial alignments. Mirrored his findings with terrifying precision.

The static on phone lines grew worse. Evening, STU-II line to NSA, a familiar noise burst. This time, beneath white noise, faint but unmistakable: chanting – multiple voices, harsh, guttural, unrecognized language, rising and falling in complex, hypnotic rhythm. Three or four seconds, then it cut off.

"Did you hear that?" Davies asked the NSA analyst.

"Hear what? Line’s noisy. Solar flares, probably."

Solar flares. It wasn't. Something else. Ancient, intelligent, bleeding through the fragile electronic membrane. He hung up, hand trembling. Opened a notebook. "Auditory Phenomena": date, time, description: Rhythmic chanting, multiple voices, unknown language, heard clearly beneath line static. Closed notebook. Thompson warned him off. Senses betraying him. But data, glitches, whispers, Angell’s echoes – terrifyingly coherent. Something fundamentally wrong. Getting closer.

### Chapter 10: Hasenfus Down

(October 5, 1986 onwards)

News crackled Sunday afternoon, October 5th: small cargo plane down in southern Nicaragua. Evening: story exploded. Sandinistas shot down American plane supplying Contras. Captured Eugene Hasenfus, American mercenary, talking – secret supply flights from Ilopango, El Salvador, managed by shadowy Americans linked to the CIA.

CIA Ops Centre teletypes chattered. News bulletins interrupted TV. For Oliver North, news hit like a physical blow. At home, the secure phone rang. Raced to OEOB. Corporate Air Services Flight 123\. He knew that plane, crew, and routes. His operation, exposed.

Office descended into frantic damage control. Phones rang. Secure telexes spewed. North, Poindexter, trusted staffers huddled. "How did they get him?" North demanded. "Sandinista air defences are minimal\!"

"Ground fire," Poindexter said calmly, though a flicker of something – concern? Annoyance? – in his eyes. "Hasenfus claims they were flying low."

"Low? Why?" North slammed fist on files. Standard procedure: higher altitude.

Pilot error? Or something else? North’s mind flashed to anomalies – glitches, headaches, nightmares. Had the crew experienced something similar? Disorientation? Equipment malfunction caused by… what? Rational explanation needed. Mechanical failure. Bad weather. Lucky shot. But the name Hasenfus kept using for his shadowy employers, "the Enterprise," had an unsettling, almost corporate ring to it, as if even the mercenaries were cogs in a vast, unseen machine.

Hasenfus’s capture: real disaster. Naming names, confirming secret supply network North built with diverted Iranian arms sales funds. Two secret operations, intertwined, now threatened with public exposure.

"Contain this," Poindexter stated. "Immediate communication blackout. Langley \- activate denial protocols. State Department \- prepare statement: no US government involvement."

North worked phones, barking orders. Rising panic, colder than simple fear. Carefully constructed reality buckling. The Symphony of Madness losing its conductor, collapsing into pure noise. Accidental? Or deliberate? Had something allowed this? The whispers in his nightmares, the formless, faceless presence he sometimes felt orchestrating events from just beyond perception – did it delight in this unravelling? He shook his head. Paranoia. Stress.

Michael Davies heard on car radio. Corporate Air Services. Recognized it. Peripherally linked in Project Nightjar – air charter service paid by "Korel Limited" shell. Secret world collided with public reality. Hasenfus incident ripped a hole in the veil, threatening to expose not just Contra supply, but entire financial architecture, including Iranian arms sales fund diversion.
