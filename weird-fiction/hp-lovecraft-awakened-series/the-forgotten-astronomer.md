# The Forgotten Astronomer

## Beat 1: Opening Image (0-1%)

In the accursed township of Wraith's End, fog wreathed the decrepit habitations like a cerement woven from the dreams of drowned gods. The year was 1928, and the blighted Massachusetts hamlet exhaled corruption. Furtive fishermen with unwholesome visages muttered of ill-omened tides whilst shunning the cyclopean lighthouse that loomed upon the cliffs like blackened obsidian. In the village square, a piscine effigy gleamed with the dark lustre of hematite, polished by clandestine worship.

## Beat 2: Theme Stated (5%)

Samuel Whitlock, astronomer and exile, entered the dockside tavern seeking provisions. The salt-scarred mariner Jedediah Crane watched him from a shadowed corner, then approached with deliberate steps.

"Professor," Crane rasped, his voice heavy with generations of ingrained dread. "Some men seek the stars for truth. But the cosmos don't care for human truth." His eyes, ancient with sorrow, fixed on Samuel with terrible knowing. "It answers questions we shouldn't ask, shows us visions we can't unsee. Your wife... she learned that, didn't she? Before she walked into the sea." He leaned closer, his breath reeking of brine and older things. "The stars summon, aye. But what comes when they're answered ain't salvation, Professor. It's the end of all we know."

Samuel recoiled, clutching his parcels. "Miriam's disappearance was a tragedy, nothing more. I'll thank you not to speak of matters you don't understand."

Crane's expression remained grave. "I understand more than you credit, Professor. We all do, in this damned place. Mind yourself when you look up at night. Some gazes look back."

## Beat 3: Setup (1-10%)

Samuel retreated to his observatory, a dome of tarnished brass perched beside the cliffs. His countenance was gaunt at fifty winters, drawn by grief for Miriam, who had vanished five years hence amidst a tempest. Her parting whisper haunted him: "The stars summon."

Once, he had taught at Miskatonic University. His theories on non-Euclidean geometries glimpsed between distant galaxies had earned him first acclaim, then suspicion, then derision. Cast out as a heretic, he had sought exile in Wraith's End, where Miriam's affection had briefly illuminated his isolation.

Now he languished among crumbling star charts and her relics: a coral necklace that thrummed against his skin with sub-audible resonance, its surface gleaming like diseased amber; a shawl retaining the scent of deep sea and lavender; a daguerreotype wherein her smile seemed to widen into a chasm. He kept a locked coffer of her unopened letters and a grey stone she'd found, etched with a spiral pattern that shifted under prolonged scrutiny. He recalled her strange humming in those final months, a resonant thrumming that vibrated in sympathy with this very stone, and her fingers tracing impossible constellations on his charts with reverent familiarity.

Once he'd found her weeping, clutching the stone. "It demands too much," she'd muttered before composing herself with a strained smile.

The townsfolk's baleful gazes reminded him uncomfortably of Miriam's own in her final days. Their whispered tales of the "Old Ones' pact" and "gifts of the deep" clung to him like the perpetual mist. He had begun researching the town archives, seeking rational explanations for the pervasive strangeness. He found unsettling sketches: piscine humanoids in ritual ecstasy, spiral patterns identical to Miriam's stone, repeated references to a "Veil" between worlds. More disturbing were the fragmented genealogies, including Miriam's ancient lineage, riddled with mentions of sea-gifts, sacred duties to the tides, and ancestors who walked into the ocean never to return.

One brittle document described the lighthouse's construction not merely as a beacon for ships, but as a ward, its light specifically angled to countervail certain influences from the void. The text hinted that its precise orientation maintained a delicate equilibrium between worlds.

## Beat 4: Catalyst (10%)

## Beat 4: Catalyst (10%)

Upon a night of unwonted clarity, the stars burning with aggressive brilliance, a vision assailed him: Miriam, her eyes aglow with phosphorescence from nethermost gulfs, beckoned toward the lighthouse with a gesture both familiar and alien. He awoke gasping. The air was thick and cold as grave-earth.

Her necklace on his nightstand emitted a sickly sweet odor alongside rhythmic luminescence, pulsing like the heartbeat of something drowned. Its coral beads possessed an opalescent sheen that shifted with nauseating fluidity. A compulsion, cold and insistent, gripped him. He found himself dressing and traversing the mist-enshrouded cliffs with steps guided by an intelligence not his own.

The lighthouse's nethermost chamber, inundated with warm brine, concealed a relic beneath mouldering timbers: a tome swathed in blackened oilskin, its surface slimy as submarine hide. The *Caelum Profundis*. The name seared his intellect like a brand. Sigils writhed under his gaze with obscene vitality.

As his fingers brushed its surface, an icy burn shot up his arm. Profound nausea washed over him. He recoiled, choking. The pages felt thin as shed skin, the script shifting and squirming, hinting at consciousness bound within the ink, a malevolent sentience that had survived aeons. He wanted to hurl it into the sea, but the necklace on his breast tightened with cold pressure, commanding him forward with irresistible compulsion.

With trembling hands, he carried it back to his observatory, the thing radiating bone-chilling cold.

## Beat 5: Debate (10-20%)

## Beat 5: Debate (10-20%)

For days, the tome lay upon his desk, radiating dread and perverse fascination. Twice, he attempted to destroy it with mounting desperation. He tried to burn it in his stove, but the flames recoiled hissing, leaving the slimy cover untouched, the air filling with an odor of ancient, stagnant water from pre-Cambrian abysses. He carried it to the cliffs to cast it into the surf, but as he raised his arm, the necklace constricted like a band of icy fire. The tome grew impossibly heavy, anchoring him until the impulse passed, replaced by unnatural calm and renewed urge to understand its secrets.

Sleep offered no escape. Visions of the tome's sigils burned behind his eyelids like molten brands, interspersed with memories of Miriam chanting in resonant tones, her eyes fixed on distant, unseen points beyond the horizon. He would awaken sweating, the urge to open it a physical clawing in his gut.

"This is madness," his rational mind screamed with diminishing strength. "Miriam is gone. This promises nothing but damnation." He tried to cling to her memory: her laughter, the warmth of her hand, their quiet evenings. He paced, reciting astronomical facts, laws of physics, anything to anchor himself. He attempted to write a letter to a colleague at Miskatonic, a desperate plea, but his hand trembled and the words became meaningless gibberish.

The whispers from tome and necklace intensified, promising answers, promising *her*, twisting his grief into a tool for their insidious purpose.

## Beat 6: Break Into Two (20%)

Finally, with a groan half surrender and half despair, he opened it with shaking hands. Within, a chart delineated the Veil of Y'thar, a constellation alien to all known cosmography. Its lines writhed like the cogitations of a deranged cosmos, each star marked with symbols that gleamed like black diamonds against an impossible void. A chilling recollection stirred: Miriam by the sea's edge, her voice unnaturally resonant, intoning a cacophonous strain. He remembered her sketching similar star charts in the sand, charts whose disturbing accuracy he had dismissed as fanciful but which now mirrored the tome's unholy diagrams with horrifying precision.

## Beat 7: B Story (22%)

Driven by terror and desperate need for understanding, Samuel sought out Crane at the tavern. He found the mariner mending nets by the docks, amidst the scent of tar and drying kelp. Crane took one look at Samuel's wild eyes and trembling hands, then led him to a quieter spot.

"Thou hast looked, haven't ye?" Crane rasped. "The Veil. She led ye to it, same as she was led by those who came before." He gestured with a hand that bore faint, web-like traces between the fingers. "My lad lies abed, coughing brine, for thy sacrilege. The Veil stirs, and the Old Night will follow if it fully wakes."

Crane leaned closer, his breath smelling of salt and something older. "We are descendants of those who made pacts, Professor. For protection and for lineage. That book you found, the *Caelum Profundis*, it was their offering to the deep. Its power is immense, a direct conduit to realms beyond mortal ken. For generations, we believed leaving it undisturbed somehow kept the worst at bay. But its true nature is that of a key, Professor. A key that only turns one way. To open it, to decipher its rites, is to unlock the door and invite the very horrors it describes."

Crane's eyes met Samuel's with terrible understanding. "You ask why I didn't do more? When my own father began to succumb, when he started speaking of the songs from the dark water, I attempted to destroy our family's copy of that damned book. It nearly took my life, and the backlash struck my boy with afflictions no doctor can name. The influence is too deeply woven into this place, into our blood, into the very stones. Your curiosity, your Miriam, she was a lightning rod. The sea-blood ran strong in her line, making her an ideal instrument."

Samuel recoiled, haunted by a litany he vaguely recalled from the town's debased chapel: "From the depths, the stars ascend." The townsfolk were not merely swayed by Miriam. They were her kin, bound by blood and ancient covenants to the abyssal powers she served.

## Beat 8: Fun and Games (20-50%)

## Beat 8: Fun and Games (20-50%)

The lighthouse possessed an old, powerful telescope in its lantern room, a relic from a previous keeper who had vanished under mysterious circumstances. It had lain disused for decades. Now, under the tome's insidious guidance and the necklace's relentless urging, frantic energy seized him.

The tome revealed a chilling passage detailing how the lighthouse's original warding energies could be re-attuned, perverted into something abominable. The delicate equilibrium the ancient texts spoke of was a lock waiting for the correct key to twist it from a shield into a gateway.

For days, he toiled in the lighthouse peak, cleaning lenses thick with salt and grime, adjusting ancient, corroded mechanisms with fevered determination. It was as if another will guided his hands, his expertise twisted to serve an alien purpose. He fought against the impulse, knowledge that he was dismantling a protective structure gnawing at his sanity. But the necklace burned with cold fire, its coral beads pulsing with rhythms that made his heart stutter in sickening sympathy.

With each adjustment to the ancient mechanisms, each careful polishing according to the tome's unholy diagrams, he felt a subtle shift in the lighthouse's atmosphere: a weakening of some unseen barrier, a growing coldness that had nothing to do with sea air. The very light that passed through the lens seemed to bend differently, the ambient hum changing from a protective resonance to a low, expectant thrum that vibrated deep within his bones.

His journal burgeoned with frenzied annotations, its pages defiled by sketches of spirals and lidless countenances, wrought in somnambulistic fervour. Yet interspersed were passages of chilling lucidity: *"October 17th: I am perverting the lighthouse. The ancient ward, I can feel it breaking. The air itself feels thinner, colder. What horror am I unleashing?"* And later: *"October 19th: Saw her in a dream again, not Miriam, but the thing she has become. It wears her face. It commands."*

His slumbers were plagued by visions of a sunken metropolis, its spires defying Euclidean precept, their surfaces encrusted with barnacles that gleamed like twisted pearls in submarine gloom. Miriam presided there, her flesh agleam with squamous lustre, her eyes radiant with the Veil's verdant malevolence. He would awaken, his reflection in the cracked mirror pale and distended, a stranger possessed.

## Beat 9: Midpoint (50%)

## Beat 9: Midpoint (50%)

With a sigh that surrendered to the inevitable, he aligned the lighthouse telescope to the Veil's coordinates. This was the final act, corrupting the ward into a beacon. The lens of the ancient instrument seemed to ripple, its glass distorting as if space itself warped under unseen gravity.

Therein he beheld not stars, but eyes: unblinking, malignant, glaring from a void where temporality was mockery and sanity forgotten luxury. Whispers as of a drowned multitude permeated the chamber, echoing Miriam's voice, now seductive, now commanding: "Gaze deeper, beloved."

For one terrible moment, Samuel felt triumph. He had found her. The Veil had answered. She was there, waiting in the depths between stars, and now he could reach her, understand what she had become.

But as Y'thar's single lidless orb pulsed through the lens, a malevolent sun in a dying universe, Samuel understood the truth. This was no rescue. This was doom. He had not found Miriam. He had summoned something infinitely worse, and she had always meant for him to do so.

The telescope's lens remained subtly contorted, a permanent scar upon the glass, forever attuned to abyssal vistas. Even when not in use, a faint hum emanated from it. On moonless nights, the air around the eyepiece shimmered with impossible colours, a conduit for whispers from beyond known spheres.

The chamber grew dank, brine seeping where no ocean could intrude. The necklace scorched his breast with unholy chill, its coral pulsing as though imbued with unhallowed life, a parasite feeding on his dwindling will.

## Beat 10: Bad Guys Close In (50-75%)

## Beat 10: Bad Guys Close In (50-75%)

The Veil waxed effulgent, its sickly green radiance a wound in the celestial vault, perceptible now even to the oblivious, pulsing with malign luminescence like radioactive jade. The tides grew perverse, surging at unnatural times with hungry malevolence. Piscine life forsook the nets in terror. The fog thickened with pervasive fetor, as if the sea itself exhaled doom.

Samuel's body began to fail him. His skin grew pallid and clammy, taking on a faint, unwholesome sheen in certain lights. He coughed brine despite never entering the water. His fingers cramped into claws when he tried to write anything but the tome's translations. The necklace had burned a permanent brand into his chest, the coral's pattern now etched into his very flesh.

Sleep brought no respite, only deeper horrors. The dreams intensified. He saw not just Miriam's sunken city, but legions of her kindred, whole bloodlines transformed and waiting in the depths. They called to him with her voice, promised him reunion in the cold embrace of Y'thar's gaze.

Worse still were the waking visions. The townspeople's faces shifted when he passed, their piscine features becoming more pronounced, their eyes gleaming with terrible knowledge. A matron clutching an infant with membranous digits hissed at him: "Avert thine eyes from the firmament, Whitlock! What lies beyond will devour all."

Crane's son died, drowning in his own bed, his lungs filled with seawater that had no earthly source. The mariner came to Samuel's observatory, grief-stricken and furious.

"The Veil hungers because of what you've done," Crane said, his voice breaking. "It takes our children first. Then it will take us all. Can you stop it, Professor? Can you undo what you've wrought?"

But Samuel had no answer. The tome's hold was absolute. Even now, it whispered of the final ritual, the lunar eclipse approaching, the moment when Y'thar could fully manifest.

Driven by desperate need to understand, Samuel deciphered more of the tome with feverish intensity. He learned the Veil was a psychic fetter for Y'thar, a Great Old One immured in stellar abyss, whose very dreams could curdle reality and whose waking would unmake existence itself. Annotations in Miriam's sinuous script prescribed a rite to unshackle the entity from its cosmic prison.

Crane's words, the fragmented histories, Miriam's humming, her sketches: it all coalesced into a dreadful, undeniable pattern. She hadn't just been influenced. She had been aware, perhaps from the start, a participant in this horror rather than its victim.

## Beat 11: All Is Lost (75%)

## Beat 11: All Is Lost (75%)

A cold dread deeper than any he had known settled in his heart. He thought of Crane's words, of generations struggling against this cosmic tide. Had Miriam known all along?

He finally forced himself to open the coffer of her letters with trembling hands. Among them, one dated shortly before her disappearance was particularly chilling. It spoke not in confession, but with cold, detached clarity:

"The call is absolute now, Samuel. The deep sings my true name, the only name that ever mattered. What I am becoming is what I was always meant to be. What I must do is inevitable as the tides. The stars they show me, they are not *your* stars. Do not seek forgiveness, for it is irrelevant. You will follow the path I set for you."

The words, devoid of any discernible human emotion, confirmed his deepest fears with brutal finality. Miriam was no simple victim, but a willing agent. Her past affection had been a calculated lure, her parting a strategic step in a horrifying design spanning generations. Her demise was a staged exodus to her true kin, leaving the necklace as a conduit for her cold machinations, a leash for his soul.

Every shared memory, every perceived tenderness, now felt like a meticulously crafted illusion. The fleeting expressions he'd mistaken for sadness were perhaps nothing more than the alien workings of a mind utterly beyond his comprehension, or the subtle shifts of a predator observing its prey.

She had never loved him. He had been a tool from the start.

## Beat 12: Dark Night of the Soul (75-80%)

## Beat 12: Dark Night of the Soul (75-80%)

Samuel collapsed at his desk, the letter crumpling in his shaking fist. The cosmic despair threatened to consume him whole. He wept, great racking sobs that echoed through the empty observatory.

He had given everything to Miriam. His career, his reputation, his sanity. He had followed her to this accursed place, endured exile and mockery, all for love. And it had been a lie. Every moment, every tender word, every shared evening gazing at stars, all of it had been manipulation. She had used him as surely as the tome now used him, as surely as Y'thar used them both.

He looked at the necklace on his desk, its coral beads gleaming with malevolent life. He should destroy it. He should flee this place, warn others, do *something*. But what was the point? Crane's son was dead. The town was doomed. The Veil pulsed in the sky like an infected wound, and he had been the scalpel that opened it.

He was insignificant. An ant moving a grain of sand on a beach of cosmic powers. The universe was vast and cold and utterly indifferent to human suffering. Miriam had understood that. She had embraced it. And now Samuel understood it too.

The knowledge was poison. It would have been kinder never to know, never to look up at the stars with anything more than childish wonder. Truth was not enlightenment. Truth was damnation.

He sat in the darkness, waiting for nothing, hoping for nothing. The astronomer who had sought to understand the cosmos had finally succeeded, and the understanding had broken him utterly.

## Beat 13: Break Into Three (80%)

## Beat 13: Break Into Three (80%)

But as the lunar eclipse approached, as the Veil's verdant blaze rent the fog and cast Wraith's End in apocalyptic glow, something shifted in Samuel's shattered mind.

If he was damned, if Miriam was truly lost to the abyss, if the world itself teetered on the brink of cosmic annihilation, then he would face it. Not as a puppet dancing on alien strings, not as a tool in some incomprehensible design, but as a man who had looked upon the truth and survived long enough to witness its culmination.

He would go to the lighthouse. He would see Miriam, or the thing she had become. He would look upon Y'thar's final manifestation with his own eyes. And if the world ended, if sanity shattered, if reality itself unmade, at least he would know. At least he would understand the full scope of his damnation.

It was not defiance. It was not hope. It was acceptance of the only path left to him.

He rose from his desk, legs unsteady, and began the walk to the lighthouse with leaden steps.

## Beat 14: Finale (80-99%)

The cliffs quaked. The sea below seethed with phosphorescent forms, grotesque amalgams of man and fish, their voices a chorus of guttural blasphemies. The matron he'd seen in the village wailed, her child's cough a liquid, gurgling lament. An elder prostrated before the piscine effigy in the square, beseeching salvation from a god that was also their progenitor.

Samuel ascended the winding stairs of the lighthouse, each step an agony. The tome felt like a block of ice in his satchel, the necklace a throbbing weight against his hammering heart. His will eroded with each whisper urging surrender.

The lighthouse's uppermost sanctum unveiled a shrine: an altar of glistening black coral and osseous relics, Miriam's sea-stained shawl draped thereupon, redolent of submarine decay and corrupted lavender.

She emerged from the gloom, no longer the woman he knew. Her mortal guise had sloughed away like shed skin to reveal something far older and infinitely more terrible. Scales shimmered with oily iridescence upon her transforming flesh, catching light like shattered opal. Her eyes blazed with the green fire of the Veil. Her voice was a gurgle from the deepest trenches where light had never penetrated.

"Thou hast beheld the stars, beloved," she intoned, the sound vibrating in his bones with unnatural resonance. "Thou hast prepared the way. Thou hast summoned Him."

"Thou hast beheld the stars, beloved," she intoned, the sound vibrating in his bones with unnatural resonance. "Thou hast prepared the way. Thou hast summoned Him."

Samuel reeled, the tome clutched against his chest. "Miriam. What have you become?" he croaked, tears veiling her monstrously beautiful form. "Our years together, was it all deception?"

Miriam's webbed claw reached for the tome, and for the briefest moment, something flickered in her blazing emerald eyes. Not sorrow, but cold calculation. "Thy devotion was useful, Samuel. It anchored you to the path I laid. It served Y'thar. And it served *me*." Her voice, a guttural rasp from the deepest trenches, held no trace of the cadence he had cherished, only chilling resonance of the abyss given form.

The restored telescope stood aimed at the Veil with terrible precision. Through its scarred lens, Y'thar's single, lidless orb pulsed, a malevolent sun in a dying universe. Its iris was a swirling vortex of impossible colours: rubies bleeding into diseased emeralds, sapphires drowning in putrid amber, all spiraling into a central pupil blacker than obsidian. Its scrutiny was physical weight, pressure that threatened to pulverize his intellect into gibbering fragments.

Y'thar's thoughts, cold and vast, inundated him. A cacophony of aeons, of realities born and consumed in cycles beyond mortal reckoning. His blood shrieked in protest. His sanity frayed like old rope stretched beyond all limits.

His soul shattered, a precipice of madness yawning before him like the maw of some cosmic predator. The psychic assault was unbearable. His mind felt flayed, his will scoured away by forces that regarded human consciousness as less than dust. The implanted commands were absolute, inescapable.

A primal terror, the last vestige of his dissolving self, sparked a final, violent convulsion through his frame. His hands, no longer his own, fumbled at his chest, tearing at the searing, burrowing presence of the necklace not in reasoned defiance, but in mindless, instinctual agony born of animal desperation. With a choked gurgle, he ripped it from his throat with savage force. It flew from his spasming grasp, striking the stone floor and shattering into a dozen dull, lifeless fragments.

The sudden cessation of the necklace's direct influence sent his body into violent, uncontrolled paroxysm while Y'thar's overwhelming psychic torrent continued unabated. He stumbled, reeling, his limbs flailing with brute force.

Miriam lunged, a guttural shriek tearing from her throat, her squamous form agleam with unholy phosphorescence, her eyes blazing with furious possessiveness, fixed on the *Caelum Profundis* still clutched in Samuel's trembling hands. "The offering!" she hissed, her alien hunger overriding all else.

The lighthouse itself gave a tremendous, stone-splintering shudder, caught in the vortex of Y'thar's burgeoning presence. As Miriam's taloned hand shot out for the tome, her lunge coincided with Samuel's violent spasm and the tower's heave in catastrophic timing. Her claws raked across Samuel's arm, drawing blood that steamed in unnatural cold. The impact jarred the *Caelum Profundis* from his nerveless grip with brutal force.

The tome spun through the air, its trajectory wild, striking the edge of the tilting profane altar and tumbling directly into the heart of flickering tapers with almost deliberate precision.

Flames, initially reluctant as if fearing the unholy text, licked at the oilskin, then caught, erupting with unnatural, green-tinged hunger that consumed not merely paper but the very essence bound within. The sigils on the pages seemed to writhe and scream, a chorus of the damned wailing as ancient power was consumed by purifying flame.

Miriam froze mid-lunge, her shriek dying in her throat, her webbed hands outstretched in futile desperation, watching the destruction with wide, unblinking eyes that reflected the inferno with terrible comprehension. Raw, alien frustration contorted her features as the offering burned.

As the last page curled into ash and the final glowing coral fragment lay inert among the ruins, a profound, shuddering change overcame her with inexorable finality.

Her form flickered, the abyssal radiance in her eyes dimming, sputtering like a faulty connection being severed by forces beyond mortal understanding. No sound of sorrow escaped her, only a dry, sibilant hiss like escaping gas from a deep fissure as the energies animating her dissipated into cold night air. The very substance of her being seemed to lose cohesion, her scaled flesh dissolving into a shimmering, saline mist that eddied for a moment with palpable frustration, then was swiftly, inexorably drawn back towards the churning, enraged sea below, reclaimed by the cold, indifferent depths she served with such terrible devotion.

The lighthouse shuddered violently. Stone fissured and groaned under supernatural strain, the profane altar crumbling into glowing embers and dust. Samuel lay insensate upon the floor, the roar of the receding tide and crackling flames a distant sound as the sea's icy embrace seemed to claim the very foundation of the tower.

The Veil above did not vanish, but its terrible luminescence pulsed, then slowly, almost imperceptibly, began to recede, its direct gaze shifting, as if an immense eye had merely blinked, its attention momentarily diverted but not extinguished, its hunger unsated and eternal.

## Beat 15: Final Image (99-100%)

## Beat 15: Final Image (99-100%)

Wraith's End lay half-submerged, its remnant denizens mute, their forms subtly, irrevocably more piscine than before. The effigy in the square had toppled, but its shadow remained long and dark in the faint, returning starlight.

Weeks hence, a mariner retrieved Samuel's journal from the debris-strewn shore, its pages warped and stained with salt and worse things. Its final coherent inscription was a single, shaky scrawl: "Miriam. Forgive me." A faint, squamous imprint like a damp handprint from some webbed appendage marred the cover.

The fog, though thinner, still enshrouded all. Yet those few who dared peer skyward, especially through glass that had once been touched by the abyss, glimpsed a verdant glimmer, faint as a moribund star, yet undeniably present, throbbing almost imperceptibly in the nocturnal void.

It was a silent promise of Y'thar's eventual, inevitable return. A chilling reminder that some gazes, once met, could never be truly broken.

The End.
